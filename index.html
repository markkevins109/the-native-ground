NATIVE GROUND

Restoring biodiversity in the heart of the Western Ghats.

Nestled on the mist-covered slopes of Wayanad, Native Ground is transforming cardamom estates into living rainforest sanctuaries within one of the world’s richest biodiversity landscapes.

Blending ecological restoration with sustainable cultivation, Native Ground represents a long-term commitment to protecting the fragile ecosystems of the Western Ghats for future generations.

Current Restoration Initiatives

Chemical-Free Cultivation

Native Ground has currently set aside 22 acres of cardamom plantation for completely chemical-free cultivation. The use of chemical pesticides and synthetic fertilizers has been fully discontinued.

Biodiversity Assessment

Baseline documentation of flora, fauna, birdlife, and ecological indicators has been initiated to guide long-term biodiversity restoration efforts and monitor ecological recovery over time.

Expert Ecological Evaluation

Wildlife experts and conservationists including Salim Pichan, Dr. Anil Zachariah, and Aravind Anil, along with their associates, visited Native Ground for a preliminary ecological evaluation. Their observations and feedback have been highly encouraging.

Native Species Restoration

With the onset of the 2026 monsoon, Native Ground will begin planting carefully selected rare and native tree species sourced from trusted conservation nurseries. Efforts are also underway to establish an in-house nursery to support future ecological restoration initiatives.

Towards a Regenerative Landscape

The long-term vision of Native Ground is to create a self-sustaining ecological landscape where rainforest biodiversity, soil regeneration, clean water systems, wildlife habitats, and sustainable cardamom cultivation coexist in harmony.

A Movement Beyond a Single Estate

Native Ground is not merely an ecological restoration project within one cardamom estate. It aspires to become a larger movement dedicated to restoring biodiversity across cardamom-growing landscapes of Kerala and the Western Ghats.

By promoting awareness, ecological responsibility, and regenerative agricultural practices, Native Ground hopes to contribute toward healthier ecosystems, healthier food systems, and healthier communities.

THE CHALLENGE

When a Rainforest Crop Turns Against the Rainforest

Cardamom — the legendary “Queen of Spices” — is native to the ancient rainforests of the Western Ghats. For centuries, it represented purity, wellness, and a harmonious relationship between cultivation and nature.

Today, that delicate balance is under severe strain. Across large plantation landscapes, increasing dependence on chemical-intensive cultivation practices is placing immense pressure on biodiversity, soil health, water systems, wildlife habitats, and human well-being.

Chemical Contamination & Ecological Decline

Excessive use of synthetic pesticides and fertilizers in cardamom cultivation has led to growing concerns over chemical residues, ecological degradation, and contamination of fragile mountain ecosystems.

These practices not only threaten rainforest biodiversity but may also affect pollinators, birds, amphibians, soil microorganisms, and streams that originate within the Western Ghats — one of the world’s most important biodiversity hotspots.

Human Health Concerns

The increasing chemical burden associated with plantation agriculture has also raised serious public health concerns for plantation workers, farming communities, and consumers.

Potential health risks associated with prolonged exposure to hazardous pesticides may include:

  • Acute pesticide poisoning
  • Respiratory illnesses
  • Reproductive and developmental disorders
  • Neurological complications
  • Musculoskeletal disorders
  • Increased long-term cancer risks

Protecting biodiversity and protecting human health are deeply interconnected challenges.

A Landscape-Level Impact

Cardamom cultivation extends across nearly 100,000 acres in Kerala alone, influencing vast ecologically sensitive regions of the Western Ghats.

Its environmental footprint affects: forest ecosystems, water sources and streams, pollinators and birdlife, plantation workers and nearby communities, and consumers across India and global export markets.

The scale of the challenge demands not isolated interventions, but a larger ecological transformation.

Economic & Global Trade Risks

International markets are increasingly enforcing stricter food safety and residue standards.

Repeated concerns over pesticide contamination and export rejections threaten not only India’s cardamom trade, but also the long-standing global reputation of one of the country’s most iconic spices.

The future sustainability of the industry may depend on transitioning toward safer, ecologically responsible cultivation systems.
