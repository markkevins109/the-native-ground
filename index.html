The Challenge

The Crisis Threatening Cardamom

Cardamom — a native of western Ghats in Kerala celebrated for centuries as the “Queen of Spices” — once symbolized purity, wellness, and the rich ecological heritage of the rainforest.

Intensive Chemical Use

Intensive chemical-dependent cultivation practices have led to pesticide residues in cardamom far exceeding internationally accepted safe limits. This growing contamination affects the health of farmers, plantation workers, local communities, supply-chain handlers, and consumers globally.

Major health issues include Acute Pesticide Poisoning (APP), Cancer, Respiratory and Reproductive Issues, Musculoskeletal Disorders, and Congenital Abnormalities.

Widespread Impact

Spread across nearly 100,000 acres in Kerala alone, cardamom cultivation directly and indirectly impacts hundreds of thousands of farm workers and millions of people who come into contact with the produce.

Increasingly stringent international quality standards and repeated rejection of export consignments are also placing severe pressure on India’s cardamom trade and global reputation.

"Our vision is to help revive a future where cardamom can once again be cultivated in harmony with nature — safe for people, sustainable for farmers, and respectful of the forests from which it originated."

Ecological Impact

Cardamom Cultivation is Damaging the Biodiversity in Western Ghats

01

Toxicity

Excessive use of pesticides and chemicals is contaminating soil and water across the Western Ghats. These toxins are affecting human health and destroying birds, insects, amphibians, and beneficial microorganisms essential for forest life.

02

Pollinator Decline

Bees and other pollinators are rapidly declining due to repeated chemical spraying, weakening the natural balance between forests, flowering plants, and wildlife.

03

Soil Death

Beneficial soil microbes that naturally maintain soil fertility are being destroyed, turning living forest soil into chemically dependent land. Many indigenous plant species have disappeared or drastically declined.

04

Climate Shift

Ecological imbalance and rising temperatures are becoming increasingly visible in the region.

05

Erosion Risk

Loss of organic matter and soil degradation have reduced the land’s natural ability to retain water. Water flows rapidly down instead of being absorbed into the soil, contributing to floods and landslides.

“If the forests of the Western Ghats disappear, the loss will not belong to Kerala alone — it will belong to humanity.”

Strategically located adjacent to the 2024 disastrous landslide area, Native Ground is envisioned as a model for ecological resilience and restoration of Biodiversity.
